Conference paper : The Future of Computing Beyond Moore's Law: Limits, New Materials, and Emerging Architectures

. 

      Moore's Law has driven exponential advances in computing for over five decades by predicting that the number of components on an integrated circuit would double roughly every two years.
      However, as silicon transistors approach atomic-scale dimensions, physical, economic and technological barriers are slowing this trend. This article examines the key factors contributing to the decline of Moore’s Law,
      including the end of Dennard scaling, increased transistor variability and the high cost of advanced lithography. It then explores emerging technologies that could sustain future performance growth,
      such as two-dimensional semiconductor materials, specialised computer architectures and improvements in algorithms and data movement efficiency. Although no single technology can fully replace classical scaling,
      the combined progress in materials science, device engineering and heterogeneous architectures offers promising pathways. These developments suggest that while Moore’s Law may be reaching its limits,
      innovation in computing is far from slowing down.
