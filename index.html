Abstract

Moore’s Law has driven exponential advances in computing for over five decades by predicting that the number of components on an integrated circuit would double roughly every two years. However, as silicon transistors approach atomic-scale dimensions, physical, economic and technological barriers are slowing this trend. This article examines the key factors contributing to the decline of Moore’s Law, including the end of Dennard scaling, increased transistor variability and the high cost of advanced lithography. It then explores emerging technologies that could sustain future performance growth, such as two-dimensional semiconductor materials, specialised computer architectures and improvements in algorithms and data movement efficiency. Although no single technology can fully replace classical scaling, the combined progress in materials science, device engineering and heterogeneous architectures offers promising pathways. These developments suggest that while Moore’s Law may be reaching its limits, innovation in computing is far from slowing down.

Introduction

Since the 1960s, the semiconductor industry has relied on a trend known as Moore’s Law: the observation that the number of components on an integrated circuit doubles roughly every two years. This scaling has enabled continuous improvements in computing performance, transforming consumer electronics, scientific computing and global communication. For decades, advances in silicon lithography made this progression predictable and economically viable. However, as transistors approach atomic-scale dimensions, the physical foundations supporting Moore’s Law are weakening. Quantum effects, increased fabrication costs and fundamental material limits now constrain further miniaturisation, leading experts to predict that Moore’s Law will plateau in the near future (Shalf, 2020).

This article explores the causes of this slowdown, including the physical limits of silicon, and examines emerging pathways such as two-dimensional semiconductors and specialised computing architectures that may sustain performance improvements beyond traditional transistor scaling (Li et al., 2019); (Leiserson et al., 2020).

Analysis and Discussion

Why Moore’s Law Is Slowing Down

Moore’s Law was enabled by the industry’s ability to continually reduce the size of silicon transistors. Smaller transistors switch faster, consume less power and allow more components to be placed on a single chip. However, scaling is now confronting unavoidable limits. The International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors (ITRS) predicted that lithographic scaling would effectively end around 2021, almost a decade sooner than earlier forecasts (Shalf, 2020). When transistor gate lengths shrink to just a few nanometres, only a small number of silicon atoms define the critical features of the device, making further miniaturisation physically impractical.

Another key factor is the breakdown of Dennard scaling, the principle that power density remains constant as transistors shrink. Dennard scaling effectively ended around 2004, leading to stagnation in clock speeds and a shift towards multi-core processors. As shown in Shalf’s analysis, increases in performance, clock frequency and core count flatten after the mid-2000s, indicating that more transistors no longer translate into proportional performance gains (Shalf, 2020). Economic limitations further heighten the challenge, as each new generation of fabrication equipment is significantly more expensive, reducing the financial benefits of further scaling and contributing to the expectation that Moore’s Law will “flatten by 2025” (Shalf, 2020).

Physical Limits of Silicon Transistors

At nanometre scales, silicon transistors face fundamental material constraints. As channels become extremely thin, electrons experience increased scattering from imperfections on the surfaces, reducing mobility and slowing current flow. The Nature article on two-dimensional semiconductors notes that even alternative bulk materials such as germanium or indium gallium arsenide encounter similar problems when reduced to nanometre thicknesses (Li et al., 2019). Imperfections, lattice vibrations and surface interactions disrupt electron movement more severely as dimensions shrink.

Quantum mechanical effects also become more pronounced at these scales. Leakage currents, variability in device behaviour and difficulties in controlling heat all make continued silicon scaling less reliable. These issues demonstrate that the slowdown of Moore’s Law is not simply an engineering problem that can be solved with better manufacturing, but a fundamental physical boundary arising from the properties of the materials involved.

Two-Dimensional Materials as a Potential Successor

One promising response to these limitations is the exploration of two-dimensional semiconductors – crystalline materials only one atom thick. Examples include molybdenum disulfide (MoS2) and tungsten diselenide (WSe2). Because these materials have extremely flat, low-defect surfaces, electrons experience less scattering, allowing for potentially faster and more efficient transistors (Li et al., 2019). Their atomic thinness also means that channels can be made much thinner than in bulk silicon while still maintaining good electronic properties, offering a route to continued device scaling.

However, Li et al. emphasise that several major research challenges must be overcome before two-dimensional materials can be used in commercial devices (Li et al., 2019). Predicting their electronic properties accurately is difficult because real materials deviate from idealised models. Growing large, uniform and defect-free sheets remains technically demanding, with many samples exhibiting grain boundaries or inconsistent thickness. In addition, assessment of device performance is complicated by inconsistent measurement techniques between laboratories. These obstacles mean that two-dimensional materials are best viewed as a long-term candidate for extending Moore’s Law rather than an immediate replacement for silicon.

Architectural and Algorithmic Solutions Beyond Moore’s Law

Even if transistor scaling slows dramatically, computing performance can still improve through innovations above the device level. Leiserson et al. argue that future gains will come from advances in algorithms, software and system architecture rather than from transistor miniaturisation alone (Leiserson et al., 2020). Modern systems increasingly employ heterogeneous architectures, combining CPUs, GPUs and specialised accelerators such as Google’s Tensor Processing Units (TPUs). These designs use transistors more efficiently by tailoring hardware to specific computational tasks, rather than relying on a single general-purpose processor for all workloads (Shalf, 2020).

Another critical insight is that data movement now consumes more energy than computation itself. Shalf shows that moving data even a few millimetres across a chip can require more energy than performing a 64-bit arithmetic operation (Shalf, 2020). This implies that future architectures must prioritise data locality, employ advanced packaging techniques such as three-dimensional integration and potentially use photonic interconnects to reduce the cost of communication. The shift towards specialised and heterogeneous architectures reflects a broader trend: as Moore’s Law slows, performance improvements increasingly depend on how efficiently data and computation are organised, rather than simply on smaller transistors.

A Post-Moore Landscape

Both the Science and Nature articles suggest that while traditional scaling is slowing, innovation in computing is far from over (Leiserson et al., 2020); (Li et al., 2019). A combination of new materials, improved transistor designs, heterogeneous architectures and algorithm-level optimisation may together sustain performance growth. However, these developments require coordinated progress across materials science, device engineering, computer architecture and software. As Shalf notes, transitions of this scale take decades, similar to the historical shift from vacuum tubes to semiconductor electronics (Shalf, 2020).

Conclusion

Moore’s Law has been central to the evolution of modern computing, enabling continuous improvements in performance, efficiency and affordability. The physical limits of silicon, the breakdown of Dennard scaling and the rising cost of fabrication now show that traditional transistor miniaturisation cannot continue indefinitely. Nevertheless, the end of Moore’s Law does not imply the end of progress. Two-dimensional semiconductors offer a possible route to further scaling, although significant scientific and manufacturing challenges must be addressed before they can be adopted in industry (Li et al., 2019). At the same time, architectural specialisation, heterogeneous accelerators and algorithmic optimisation provide practical strategies for improving performance without relying solely on smaller transistors (Leiserson et al., 2020). Together, these developments suggest that the future of computing will be shaped by a combination of materials innovation, system-level design and software advancements rather than by transistor scaling alone.
