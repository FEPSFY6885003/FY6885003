Conference paper : The Future of Computing Beyond Moore's Law: Limits, New Materials, and Emerging Architectures

. 

      Moore's Law has driven exponential advances in computing for over five decades by predicting that the number of components on an integrated circuit would double roughly every two years.
      However, as silicon transistors approach atomic-scale dimensions, physical, economic and technological barriers are slowing this trend. This article examines the key factors contributing to the decline of Moore’s Law,
      including the end of Dennard scaling, increased transistor variability and the high cost of advanced lithography. It then explores emerging technologies that could sustain future performance growth,
      such as two-dimensional semiconductor materials, specialised computer architectures and improvements in algorithms and data movement efficiency. Although no single technology can fully replace classical scaling,
      the combined progress in materials science, device engineering and heterogeneous architectures offers promising pathways. These developments suggest that while Moore’s Law may be reaching its limits,
      innovation in computing is far from slowing down.

Why Moore's Law Is Slowing Down The original driver of Moore’s Law was the industry’s ability to continually reduce the size of silicon transistors. Smaller transistors switch faster, consume less power and allow more components to be placed on a single chip. However, as lithography approaches atomic scales, this approach is reaching unavoidable limits. According to the International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors (ITRS), lithographic scaling was predicted to end by around 2021 — nearly a decade earlier than originally expected (Shalf, 2020). When transistor gate lengths shrink to just a few nanometres, only a dozen or so silicon atoms define the critical features of the device, meaning that further miniaturisation becomes physically impractical. A second factor is the breakdown of Dennard scaling, the principle that power density remains constant as transistors shrink. Dennard scaling ended around 2004, leading to stagnation in clock speeds and a shift towards multi-core processors, as shown in Figure 2 of Shalf’s article, where increases in performance, clock frequency and core count flatten after the mid-2000s. This means that even if more transistors can be added, they no longer provide proportional performance gains. Economic limitations exacerbate the problem. Each new generation of semiconductor fabrication equipment increases in cost, making traditional scaling financially unsustainable. As a result, Moore’s Law is expected to “flatten by 2025,” with diminishing returns from further investment in silicon technology (Shalf, 2020).
